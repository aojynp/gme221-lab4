Similarly, a fixed distance band creates a "one-size-fits-all" radius that may capture dozens of neighbors in a subdivision but leave a large forest parcel isolated. Therefore, contiguity ensures the model reflects the actual topological connectivity of the of the parcels and also the landscape, where the most intense spatial autocorrelation occurs between those who are literally side-by-side.

2. # Global Autocorrelation Reflection
A positive Moran’s I, like the exercise result of 0.317, indicate spatial clustering, which means that similar values (either high or low) are located near each other in space, suggesting that "like attracts like." A value near zero suggests spatial randomness, or a lack of spatial autocorrelation. In this scenario, the value of a variable at one location provides no information about the values of its neighbors. It implies that the spatial distribution of your data is randomly determined and shows no discernible pattern of clustering or dispersion.

Moran’s I can be influenced by random chance, the p-value is essential to determine statistical significance. It shows the probability that the observed spatial pattern could have emerged from a completely random distribution. Without a p-value below a certain threshold, there is no guarantee to fully reject the null hypothesis of spatial randomness. In the lab exer 4, it resulted to 0.001 indicates that your results are highly statistically significant. This means there is only a 0.1% chance that the spatial clustering or dispersion occurred by pure random coincidence. Since this value is well below the standard threshold of 0.05, it is significant to  reject the null hypothesis of spatial randomness. Essentially, it confirms that the pattern in your data is a real geographic phenomenon rather than a fluke of the dataset.

The role of the attribute in computing Moran’s I provides the y-values (the numeric data) used to measure similarity between locations. While the spatial weights matrix (W) defines "who" the neighbors are, the attribute defines "what" is being compared. Moran’s I calculates the covariance between an attribute at a location and its the average attribute value of its neighbors.

Result of a spatial autocorrelation analysis is highly sensitive to the specific attribute chosen because different variables are driven by distinct geographic processes, sometimes also with economic processes. While one attribute might be influenced by regional factors such as watershed management, another might be governed by hyper-local conditions individual ownership on the same area of jurisdiction (usually from the LGUs). The spatial pattern of the data is not directly focused on the fixed property of the parcels but a reflection of the measurement of specific phenomenon.

Moran’s I fundamentally requires both a spatial weights matrix and an attribute variable because it measures the correlation between a location’s value and the values of its neighbors. The spatial weights matrix serves as a map for the statistical analysis, establishing the geographic structure by identifying which units are officially considered neighbors based on shared boundaries or proximity. Without this matrix, the model has no way of knowing which data points are adjacent. Conversely, the attribute variable provides the "content" for the analysis, offering the actual numerical values within that are being compared across that geographic structure.Moran’s I essentially overlaps geographic structure with data values to determine if locations that are "neighbors" (spatial weights) also share similar characteristics or the attributes. Without the spatial weights matrix, there are data points with no locational context, and without the attribute, there will only be a map with nothing to analyze. By integrating both, the statistic can distinguish between a meaningful spatial pattern and a purely random distribution of values.

3. #  Spatial Autocorrelation
Global Moran’s I measures the overall spatial autocorrelation of the entire study area, providing a single value that indicates whether the dataset is generally clustered, dispersed, or randomly distributed. On the other hand, Local Moran’s I examines spatial autocorrelation of each individual parcels, allowing the identification of specific locations where significant clustering or spatial outliers occur. Thus, Global Moran’s I gives a overall pattern on the overview, while Local Moran’s I reveals detailed, location-specific spatial structures.

Hotspots and coldspots are identified using both the Local Moran’s I value and its corresponding p-value. A positive Local Moran’s I value indicates that a parcel is surrounded by similar values, forming a cluster. If a parcel has a high value and is surrounded by high values, and the result is statistically significant where p-value < 0.05), it is classified as a hotspot (red). And, if a parcel has a low value surrounded by low values and is statistically significant, it is classified as a coldspot (blue). Parcels with non-significant p-values are labeled as not significant (light grey), meaning no meaningful spatial clustering is detected.

In the map, hotspots are primarily concentrated in the central and lower-right part of the study area, particularly along blocks and connected road networks. This pattern suggests clusters of high values in more developed or densely built-up areas. These hotspots may be due to by urban factors residential density, proximity to main roads, better accessibility, and the presence of commercial or economically active.   While the Coldspots in the dataset are mainly located on the left side of the map, where blue parcels form a continuous cluster. These areas indicate groups of low values surrounded by similarly low-value neighbors. Such patterns may be due less fewer developments, or the presence of non-urban land uses which tend to have lower assessed values compared to more built-up areas.

For the lightgrey ones or the not significant, these appear to differ from their surrounding neighbors, suggesting possible spatial outliers. These would typically show up as isolated parcels with values that contrast sharply with adjacent ones, such as a high-value parcel surrounded by low values or vice versa. In the dataset, these are less prominent but exist along transition zones between hotspot and coldspot areas wherein land use or development intensity changes abruptly.

Changing the spatial weights method directly alters how "neighborhoods" are defined, which in turn shifts the identified hotspots and coldspots. For instance, switching to KNN forces a uniform number of neighbors for every parcel, potentially creating more consistent clusters, but with distance-based method, these rely strictly hence it shows physical proximity. Consequently, these boundaries may expand, shrink, or relocate because the statistical significance of a cluster depends entirely on who is considered a neighbor.

Also the hotspots vary when running Local Moran’s I using different attributes such as ass_ass_va and ass_market. This is because each attribute represents a different aspect of value which may not be distributed the same way across the dataset. Market values may reflect current demand and accessibility more strongly, while assessed values may follow administrative or standardized valuation rules. These differences can lead to variations in where clusters of high or low values appear across the study area.
